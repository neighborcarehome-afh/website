Our Approach

Understanding the Cost of Care

At Neighbor Care Homes, every prospective resident receives a comprehensive Registered Nurse assessment prior to admission. This required assessment helps us understand your loved one's unique needs, determine the appropriate level of care, and confirm our home is the right fit for your family.

Because every resident is different, monthly rates are based on the level of care, supervision, and caregiver support required — not simply a medical diagnosis or age. If you're unsure which level of care is appropriate, we're always happy to answer your questions and help you explore the best options for your loved one.

Our Nursing Assessment

How We Determine a Resident's Level of Care

Every resident is unique. Our goal isn't simply to assign a price — it's to ensure each resident receives the level of care that allows them to live safely, comfortably, and with dignity. Our care levels reflect the overall level of care and supervision required, not any single diagnosis.

  • Assistance with bathing, dressing, grooming & toileting
  • Mobility and transfer assistance
  • Medication administration & management
  • Memory impairment or dementia
  • Wandering or exit-seeking behaviors
  • Fall risk
  • Diabetes management & Nurse Delegation services
  • Catheter, ostomy, or feeding tube care
  • Incontinence care
  • Nighttime assistance or monitoring
  • Behavioral support and redirection
  • Overall caregiver time and supervision required
Our Commitment

Our Commitment to Families

We understand that a resident's care needs may change over time, and we also recognize that temporary illnesses, recovery from hospitalization, or other short-term setbacks do not always represent a permanent change in condition.

Before recommending a change in a resident's level of care or monthly rate, we carefully evaluate whether increased care needs represent a lasting change rather than a temporary illness or recovery period.

If a resident's care needs change over time, we will always discuss our assessment, our recommendations, and the reason for any proposed adjustment with the resident and family before changes to the monthly rate take effect. Whenever possible, we provide at least 30 days' notice, allowing families time to plan, ask questions, and prepare for the transition.

We are committed to providing exceptional care with honesty, transparency, and respect, so you can spend less time worrying about billing and more time focusing on your loved one.

Planning Ahead

What If Circumstances Change?

Many families ask what happens if a resident's financial situation changes after moving into one of our homes.

Neighbor Care Homes was intentionally designed to provide a higher level of support than is typically found in many residential care settings. Our care model includes Registered Nurse oversight, 24-hour awake caregivers, enhanced staffing, personalized care planning, and close coordination with healthcare providers. Maintaining this level of care requires a financial model that allows us to consistently invest in our residents, our caregivers, and the quality of services we provide.

At the same time, we understand that financial circumstances can change over the years. To help balance long-term sustainability with our desire to continue supporting residents who have made our home their home, each Neighbor Care Home may transition one resident to Medicaid after a minimum of three years of private-pay residency, provided Medicaid funding is available and the resident continues to be appropriate for our home.

This approach allows us to preserve the high standard of care our residents and families have come to expect while also creating an opportunity for long-term residents to remain in the home they know and love if their financial circumstances change.

Exploring Your Options

Medicaid & Long-Term Care Options

Finding the right care for a loved one can feel overwhelming, especially when navigating the financial aspects of long-term care. While Neighbor Care Homes is primarily a private-pay community, we want every family to have access to helpful information and trusted resources, regardless of where they ultimately receive care.

If private-pay care is not the right fit for your family, Washington State offers long-term care Medicaid programs that may help cover the cost of care for individuals who qualify based on their financial situation and care needs.

A great place to begin is with Washington State Home and Community Services (HCS) through the Department of Social and Health Services (DSHS). HCS can help determine eligibility for long-term care Medicaid, explain available programs, and guide families through the application process.

Other Topics We Can Help With

How do I know when it's time for assisted living or an Adult Family Home?Common signs families notice first
+

Common signs include needing regular help with bathing, dressing, or medications; recent falls or hospitalizations; missed or mismanaged medications; a noticeable decline in home upkeep or personal hygiene; increasing isolation; or a family caregiver who is feeling overwhelmed or burned out. None of these on its own means a move is required, but together they're worth a conversation. Our free RN assessment can help clarify what level of support is actually needed.

Washington State Community Living Connections gives a free, no-obligation second opinion and can connect you with your local Area Agency on Aging.
1-855-567-0252 · waclc.org

Understanding dementia and memory careWhat changes as dementia progresses
+

Dementia is a general term for a decline in memory and thinking that's severe enough to affect daily life; Alzheimer's disease is its most common cause. As it progresses, families often see more wandering or exit-seeking behavior, disorientation, difficulty recognizing loved ones, and a growing need for hands-on supervision and cueing — which is when a dedicated memory care setting, like our Level 4 Comprehensive Care, tends to become appropriate.

The Alzheimer's Association Washington State Chapter offers local support groups, education, and a free 24/7 dementia helpline in over 200 languages.
1.800.272.3900 · alz.org/alzwa

Long-term care insurance resourcesIncluding Washington's own public benefit
+

If your loved one has a private long-term care insurance policy, check the daily/monthly benefit amount, the waiting period before benefits start, and whether Adult Family Home care is a covered setting. Washington also runs its own public long-term care benefit, WA Cares Fund — workers who've contributed through payroll deductions can access a lifetime benefit (currently $36,500, adjusted for inflation) that can help pay for care like ours once they qualify.

WA Cares Fund (Washington State DSHS & Employment Security Department) has eligibility details and how to apply for benefits.
wacaresfund.wa.gov

Veterans benefits (Aid & Attendance)A VA pension benefit many families miss
+

Aid & Attendance is a tax-free VA pension increase for wartime veterans, or their surviving spouses, who need help with everyday activities like bathing, dressing, or eating, or who are otherwise housebound. It isn't limited to combat veterans or nursing home residents, and the funds can be put toward Adult Family Home care.

The Washington State Department of Veterans Affairs (WDVA) provides free, one-on-one help filing Aid & Attendance and other VA claims through local Veterans Service Officers.
1-800-562-2308 · dva.wa.gov

Hospital discharge planningMoving safely from hospital to home
+

When a loved one is hospitalized, Medicare requires the hospital to screen for discharge planning needs and help arrange safe next steps — whether that's home health, a rehab stay, or a residential care setting like ours. Ask to speak with the hospital's discharge planner or social worker early in the stay. We regularly coordinate directly with hospital discharge teams for residents moving into our home.

If you feel a discharge is happening too soon or have concerns about care after leaving a Washington facility, the Washington State Long-Term Care Ombudsman is a free, independent advocate for residents and families.
waombudsman.org

Choosing the right level of careMatching support to actual needs
+

The right level of care depends on how much help someone needs with daily activities, supervision, and medical support — not on age or a single diagnosis. We walk every family through this with a comprehensive RN assessment before admission, which is how we determine which of our five care levels fits your loved one, and it's reassessed any time needs change.

See our full breakdown of how we determine level of care above. You can also verify any Washington Adult Family Home's license and inspection history through DSHS Residential Care Services.
dshs.wa.gov — Residential Care Services

Caregiver support groupsYou don't have to do this alone
+

Caring for an aging parent or spouse is emotionally and physically demanding, and support groups — in person or online — are one of the most effective ways caregivers avoid burnout. Groups exist for general family caregiving as well as disease-specific situations like dementia or Parkinson's.

Washington's Family Caregiver Support Program, run through DSHS and your local Area Agency on Aging, offers free support groups, training, counseling, and even respite care for unpaid family caregivers statewide.
dshs.wa.gov — Caregiver Resources
